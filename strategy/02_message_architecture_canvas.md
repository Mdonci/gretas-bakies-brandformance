# 02 message architecture canvas

## role of the message architecture

This canvas translates the insight into a usable message system for website copy, social bios, captions, ads, packaging, product pages, gifting pages, and outreach. The message should sound like Greta's Bakies: warm, specific, ingredient-aware, local, and never overblown.

## core message

**Gift-ready thick cookie boxes, baked in Irvine with crisp edges, dense fudgy centers, and real ingredients you can taste.**

## expanded core message

Greta's Bakies makes 4oz thick cookie boxes for Orange County gifting, sharing, and serious cravings. Each cookie is baked in small batches from an Irvine home kitchen, built around a dense fudgy center, and packed to feel thoughtful when you hand it over.

## one-line positioning

**Irvine-baked 4oz thick cookies for host gifts, office treats, Friday nights, and the people who want the middle to be the main event.**

## pillar 1: thick cookies with a real center

### pillar message

Greta's cookies are 4oz, thick, and built around the center: crisp edges, a dense fudgy middle, and melty mix-ins that make the cookie feel like dessert.

### why it matters

The research shows texture is the main shortcut customers use to judge premium cookies. They talk about crispy outside, gooey inside, fudgy consistency, melty centers, huge cookies, and warm cookies. Greta's should lead with texture before any abstract "gourmet" language.

### tension and belief reference

- Tension: "Will these have the texture I am craving, or will they be dry, cakey, or too sweet?"
- Belief shift: from "premium cookies are mostly hype" to "the value is visible when the cookie has real weight and a serious center."

### sub-messages

1. **Every cookie is 4oz and thick.** No tiny-cookie surprise. Show the size, side profile, and box count clearly.
2. **The middle is the main event.** Dense, fudgy centers should show up in photos, product descriptions, inserts, and captions.
3. **Texture beats hype.** Use words customers already use: crisp edges, gooey middle, fudgy center, melty chocolate, warm it up and split it open.
4. **Best enjoyed warm.** Do not promise hot pickup unless true. Promise a warm-at-home moment with reheating guidance.

### proof points

- 4oz cookie format
- 1.5 to 2 inch thick profile
- split-open photography and video
- reheating card in every box
- customer-facing texture words, not vague premium language

### sample lines

- "Crisp edges. Dense fudgy centers. Baked in Irvine."
- "For the gooey-middle cookie people."
- "A 4oz thick cookie built around the center."
- "Warm it up, split it open, and let the middle do its thing."
- 'Thick and dense and fudgy where it counts.'

## pillar 2: gift-ready without the extra work

### pillar message

Greta's boxes are made to be handed over as-is: polished, personal, clearly labeled, and ready for dinner parties, office treats, birthdays, thank-yous, teacher gifts, and holiday moments.

### why it matters

The strongest purchase occasion is gifting. Buyers are not only buying cookies. They are trying to avoid showing up empty-handed, generic, or underprepared. The box needs to reduce gift anxiety before the first bite.

### tension and belief reference

- Tension: "Will this look thoughtful enough for a host, client, teacher, or friend?"
- Belief shift: from "cookies are an easy backup" to "cookies can be the gift."

### sub-messages

1. **No repacking needed.** The box should look ready when the customer picks it up.
2. **Occasion-first shopping.** Build message lanes around host gifts, office treats, Friday nights, birthdays, thank-yous, teacher gifts, Friendsgiving, and holiday boxes.
3. **Small details make it feel thoughtful.** Flavor card, allergen card, reheating note, and optional handwritten message are not operational extras. They are conversion proof.
4. **The box should feel generous.** Premium price works when the customer can see weight, polish, and purpose.

### proof points

- gift-ready boxes
- optional handwritten note
- flavor labels
- allergen information
- reheating guidance
- box sizes for 4-pack, 6-pack, 12-pack, and seasonal gifting

### sample lines

- "Host gift solved."
- "Dessert that looks as thoughtful as it tastes."
- "Gift-ready cookie boxes baked in Irvine."
- "No repacking, no plating panic. Just a polished cookie box."
- "Choose your box, pick your pickup window, show up with dessert handled."

## pillar 3: ingredient-conscious indulgence

### pillar message

These are rich, indulgent cookies made with a more thoughtful ingredient standard: real butter, real chocolate, real mix-ins, and minimal artificial color.

### why it matters

The target buyer reads labels and has chain-cookie fatigue. She still wants dessert, but she wants to feel better about the ingredients and the taste balance. Since Greta's is not certified organic, the message must be concrete, not inflated.

### tension and belief reference

- Tension: "What am I actually eating, and will it be too sweet or artificial?"
- Belief shift: from "thick cookies can be too sweet or too much" to "rich can still be balanced."

### sub-messages

1. **Dessert, not a health claim.** The tone should be honest: indulgent, ingredient-conscious, and baked with care.
2. **Specific ingredients create trust.** Name real butter, real chocolate, toasted nuts, sea salt, brown butter, espresso, and other concrete flavor drivers when applicable.
3. **Minimal artificial color is a differentiator.** Say "only Red Velvet uses added color" if operationally true.
4. **Balanced, not bland.** The promise is not less flavor. It is rich flavor that is not one-note sweet.

### proof points

- real butter
- real chocolate
- real mix-ins
- minimal artificial coloring
- only Red Velvet uses added color
- home kitchen baking, real batch control
- flavor-led menu instead of color-led gimmicks

### sample lines

- "Real butter. Real chocolate. Real thick cookies."
- "Indulgent, ingredient-conscious, and baked to order."
- "Decadent, not one-note sweet."
- "Premium mix-ins, minimal artificial color, maximum cookie satisfaction."
- "Clean-leaning indulgence without pretending dessert is a salad."

## pillar 4: Irvine-baked, easy to order, local pickup

### pillar message

Greta's brings the thick-cookie craving closer to home with small-batch boxes baked in Irvine, clear pre-order timing, local pickup, and an experience that feels personal without being confusing.

### why it matters

Localness is an advantage only if it also feels reliable. Chain competitors win on convenience. Shipped competitors win on polish. Greta's must make the local home-kitchen model feel clear, fresh, and gift-worthy.

### tension and belief reference

- Tension: "Can I order this easily and get it when I need it?"
from "local home baker might be casual" to "home kitchen can still be polished"

### sub-messages

1. **Irvine is part of the value.** Use Irvine-baked, OC-made, local pickup, and Orange County cookie boxes consistently.
2. **Fresh beats shipped when timing matters.** Position local pickup as the answer for host gifts, office mornings, Friday nights, and holiday windows.
3. **Ordering should be screenshot-simple.** Cutoff, pickup windows, delivery radius, payment, cancellation, and allergen details should be clear before checkout.
4. **Small-batch means limited, not chaotic.** Use limited weekly bake language with predictable pickup windows.

### proof points

- California Cottage Food Act Class A home-kitchen model
- direct-to-consumer ordering
- Irvine pickup
- local delivery when available
- weekly pre-order windows
- consistent menu and box sizes

### sample lines

- "Baked in Irvine, picked up fresh in Irvine."
- "OC cookie boxes with a homemade heart and gift-box finish."
- "Local pickup. Fresh boxes. Thick cookies."
- "Small-batch cookies from an Irvine home kitchen."
- "Reserve your box before the weekly cutoff."

## brand voice

### voice traits

**Warm**  
The brand should feel like a thoughtful local baker, not a corporate dessert company.

**Specific**  
Say 4oz, dense fudgy center, real butter, Irvine pickup, flavor card, reheating instructions. Specifics create trust.

**Polished but not stiff**  
The buyer wants a box that looks good enough to gift, but the copy should still feel human and easy.

**Ingredient-aware**  
Acknowledge the buyer's label-reading habit without making dessert sound medical, moral, or restrictive.

**Local**  
Use Irvine and Orange County naturally. Localness should feel like freshness, care, and community, not a forced badge.

### what to sound like

- "Baked in Irvine for the gooey-middle people."
- "Add a note and we'll tuck it into the box."
- "Best enjoyed warm, especially if you want the center soft and fudgy."
- "A cookie box that feels generous before anyone takes a bite."
- "Small-batch cookies with gift-box polish."

### what not to sound like

- "The ultimate cookie experience"
- "Life-changing gourmet indulgence"
- "Guilt-free clean cookies"
- "Better than Crumbl"
- "Organic cookies" unless certified
- "Always warm" unless the pickup experience guarantees it

### words to use carefully

- gourmet, only when supported by specifics
- clean, only as clean-leaning or ingredient-conscious, not as a health claim
- Levain-style, internal category language only unless the owner chooses to use it sparingly
- natural, only if true for the ingredient being described

### words to avoid

- organic
- healthy
- guilt-free
- chemical-free
- preservative-free unless verified
- best in OC unless backed by a real award or testimonial
- low-calorie
- diet-friendly

## message rules

1. Lead with texture or occasion, not vague quality.
2. Make the 4oz size visible anywhere price appears.
3. Treat packaging as part of the product promise.
4. Use ingredient language that is concrete and compliant.
5. Answer pickup and availability questions before the customer has to ask.
6. Keep chain contrast subtle. Do not attack competitors.
7. Put Irvine and Orange County into searchable copy naturally.
8. Tie every seasonal drop to a real use case: host gift, office box, teacher gift, Friday night, Friendsgiving, holiday.
