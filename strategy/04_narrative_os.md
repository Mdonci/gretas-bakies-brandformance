# 04 narrative os

## purpose

This document is the operating system for the Greta's Bakies brand narrative. It defines the rules, the content skeleton, channel roles, and traceability standards that keep every piece of copy, every post, every product page, every box insert, and every outreach message connected to the same insight, the same pillars, and the same conversion arc. Use this as the reference before writing or approving any customer-facing content.

## section 1: operating rules

These are the principles that govern how the Greta's Bakies narrative gets told across every channel. They are not suggestions. They are the rules.

### rule 1: the buyer tension opens every story

Every piece of content, from an Instagram caption to a product page to an outreach email, should acknowledge the buyer's real situation before talking about the cookies. The situation is: she needs dessert that feels thoughtful without becoming a project. She is filtering for texture, presentation, ingredients, and ease.

**What this looks like:** Lead with the occasion, the tension, or the texture craving. Do not lead with the brand name, the founder story, or a generic "we make cookies" statement.

**Example (correct):** "Walking into dinner with a host gift that feels thoughtful and took zero baking."

**Example (incorrect):** "Greta's Bakies is a premium cookie company based in Irvine."

### rule 2: texture leads, everything else supports

The buyer's number one decision shortcut is texture. Crisp edges. Dense fudgy center. Melty chocolate. Thick. 4oz. These words must appear in the first two lines of any primary content piece (hero, bio, product intro, ad headline). Abstract quality language ("gourmet," "premium," "delicious") is never the lead. It may appear as support only after texture has been established.

**What this looks like:** The cookie's physical attributes (size, weight, center texture, edge) are stated in specific, sensory language before any quality claim.

**Example (correct):** "4oz thick cookies with crisp edges and a dense fudgy center."

**Example (incorrect):** "Delicious gourmet cookies made with premium ingredients."

### rule 3: packaging is a product promise, not an operational detail

The gift-ready box, flavor card, allergen card, reheating instructions, and optional handwritten note are conversion proof. They answer the buyer's unspoken question: "Will this look thoughtful enough to hand over?" Packaging details should be treated with the same weight as flavor and texture details.

**What this looks like:** Mention packaging specifics whenever the use case is gifting. Show the box in photography. State what is included.

### rule 4: ingredient language is concrete and compliant

Use "real butter," "real chocolate," "real mix-ins," "minimal artificial color," "only Red Velvet uses added color." These are specific, verifiable, and do not overpromise. Never use "organic," "all-natural," "chemical-free," "guilt-free," "healthiest," or "cleanest" as standalone claims. The brand is ingredient-conscious, not health-positioned.

**What this looks like:** Name ingredients when they earn it. Brown butter, sea salt, toasted nuts, dark chocolate, espresso. But do not make dessert sound like a supplement.

### rule 5: localness is freshness and credibility, not a badge

Irvine and Orange County should appear naturally in copy as part of the value: local pickup, fresh-baked, not shipped across the country, OC-made for OC occasions. Do not force "Irvine" into every sentence. Do not use localness to suggest superiority over competitors.

**What this looks like:** "Baked in Irvine, picked up fresh in Irvine" or "OC cookie boxes for host gifts and Friday nights."

### rule 6: every piece of content answers an objection before it is asked

Refer to the purchase friction table in the insight builder and the objection resolvers in the conversion narrative. Before publishing any content, check: does this answer at least one of the buyer's unspoken questions about price, texture, sweetness, presentation, pickup, freshness, or allergens?

**What this looks like:** A caption about a new flavor should also mention the 4oz size. A post about holiday gifting should also mention the gift-ready box and pickup window.

### rule 7: occasions are the organizing principle

Content is organized around use cases, not around flavors or production updates. The primary occasions are: host gifts, office treats, Friday nights and personal cravings, seasonal gifting (Friendsgiving, holidays, teacher appreciation), birthdays and thank-yous.

**What this looks like:** A content calendar grouped by occasion lanes, with flavors and box sizes as the variables within each lane. A flavor launch fits into an occasion lane, not the other way around.

### rule 8: the narrative has a spine, not a script

The conversion narrative (act 1 through act 4) is the emotional spine of all content. Every channel tells different parts of it depending on where the audience member is in the funnel. But the emotional progression (tension, discovery, conviction, loyalty) should be recognizable across the brand. Nobody should encounter Greta's Bakies and wonder what problem the brand is solving.

### rule 9: show the middle

Every product photo set must include at least one split-open shot showing the dense fudgy center. Every video should show a cookie being split or warmed. The center is the brand's primary visual proof. Social feeds, product pages, ads, and packaging imagery should all prioritize the split-open moment.

### rule 10: close with a path

Every content piece should give the audience a next step that matches their funnel position. Awareness content should invite following, saving, or sharing. Consideration content should link to the menu or the pickup details. Decision content should link to order. Post-purchase content should invite reviews and referrals.

## section 3: channel roles

Each channel has a specific job in the narrative. Do not ask a channel to do everything. Assign content to the channel whose role it matches.

### instagram

**Role:** primary awareness and consideration engine. Texture proof, local discovery, occasion framing, and social proof.

**What lives here:**
- Split-open videos and texture close-ups (highest priority).
- Box reveals and packaging posts.
- Occasion-based carousels (host gift, Friday night, office treats).
- Flavor spotlights with ingredient callouts.
- Customer resharing and review highlights.
- Pre-order window reminders in Stories.
- Local Irvine and OC content for discoverability.

**What does not live here:**
- Long-form brand storytelling.
- Detailed ordering instructions (link to website or highlights).
- Competitor comparisons or contrarian framing.
- Founder-centric content without a buyer hook.

**Narrative act focus:** act 1 (problem recognition) and act 2 (discovery and consideration).

**Voice on this channel:** warm, specific, visually-led. Captions should open with texture or occasion, not brand name. The first line of every caption should earn the stop.

### website

**Role:** decision engine and credibility anchor. The place where consideration becomes purchase. Must answer every objection and make ordering feel simple.

**What lives here:**
- Hero copy: thick 4oz cookies, gift-ready boxes, Irvine-baked, real ingredients.
- Sub-hero: packaging details, pickup clarity, occasion framing.
- Product pages with flavor descriptions, mix-in details, 4oz callout, box sizes, pricing.
- Gifting page organized by occasion.
- Pickup and delivery FAQ.
- Allergen and ingredient information.
- Order flow with clear cutoff, window, and confirmation.

**What does not live here:**
- Lifestyle photography that does not show the product.
- Vague quality language without specifics.
- Hidden pricing or pickup details that require clicking through multiple pages.

**Narrative act focus:** act 3 (decision and purchase).

**Voice on this channel:** polished but direct. Specifics over superlatives. Every section should answer a question the buyer is asking.

### email and dm

**Role:** relationship and reorder engine. The channel for post-purchase follow-up, seasonal reminders, restock alerts, and direct buyer conversation.

**What lives here:**
- Post-purchase follow-up: "how did the box go over?"
- Seasonal drop announcements with early access for past buyers.
- Reorder reminders before Friendsgiving, holidays, teacher appreciation.
- Flavor restock alerts.
- Review and referral requests.
- Direct responses to buyer questions and feedback.

**What does not live here:**
- Daily promotional blasts.
- Generic newsletter content without a specific occasion hook.
- Long-form content better suited to the website.

**Narrative act focus:** act 4 (commitment, loyalty, and referral).

**Voice on this channel:** personal and warm, as if from a baker who remembers your last order. Use "you" naturally. Reference the occasion the buyer last ordered for.

### packaging (physical box and insert)

**Role:** commitment proof and referral trigger. The packaging is the product experience that happens in the recipient's hands. It must reinforce every promise the narrative made.

**What lives here:**
- Gift-ready box with clean, branded presentation.
- Flavor card identifying each cookie.
- Allergen card with clear labeling.
- Reheating instructions for the warm-at-home moment.
- Optional handwritten note.
- Thank-you message and review request.
- Handle or QR code for reorder and referral.

**What does not live here:**
- Dense brand storytelling.
- Upsell language that feels transactional.
- Packaging that requires the buyer to repack before gifting.

**Narrative act focus:** act 4 (handoff confidence, texture payoff, and referral).

**Voice on this channel:** grateful and helpful. The insert should feel like a note from a thoughtful baker, not a terms-and-conditions document.

### outreach (office, corporate, realtor, community)

**Role:** awareness and decision for group-buying occasions. This is where Greta's Bakies enters as a solution for offices, teams, client gifts, realtor open houses, and community events.

**What lives here:**
- Office and corporate cookie box descriptions.
- Pricing for 6-pack, 12-pack, and custom box sizes.
- Ordering process and lead time for group orders.
- Gift-ready messaging with allergen and labeling details.
- Seasonal outreach before holidays, admin appreciation, and end-of-year gifting.

**What does not live here:**
- Casual social content.
- Founder story without a buyer-relevant hook.
- Pricing that is not clearly tied to box size and cookie count.

**Narrative act focus:** act 1 (problem recognition for group occasions) and act 3 (decision for group orders).

**Voice on this channel:** polished, clear, and easy to forward. The outreach message should be something a manager can paste into a team chat or an assistant can forward to a vendor without editing.

## section 4: traceability rules

Every customer-facing asset must trace back to the insight, a MAC pillar, and a belief shift. This ensures the narrative stays coherent even when different people write different pieces.

### the traceability chain

```
customer-facing asset
    ↓
which content type (from the skeleton table)?
    ↓
which MAC pillar does this activate?
    ↓
which belief shift does this support?
    ↓
which buyer tension does this resolve?
    ↓
which insight does this prove?
```

### traceability examples

**Example 1: a split-open video on Instagram**

- Content type: split-open texture video.
- MAC pillar: pillar 1 (the thickness).
- Belief shift: from "premium cookies are mostly hype" to "the value is visible when the cookie has real weight and a serious center."
- Buyer tension: "Will these have the texture I'm craving?"
- Insight: texture is the main shortcut customers use to judge premium cookies.

**Example 2: a holiday host gift announcement email**

- Content type: seasonal occasion announcement.
- MAC pillar: pillar 2 (gift-ready).
- Belief shift: from "cookies are an easy backup" to "cookies can be the gift."
- Buyer tension: "Will this look thoughtful enough for a host who knows food?"
- Insight: a premium cookie box is a low-effort way to show taste.

**Example 3: the website pickup FAQ**

- Content type: pickup and delivery FAQ.
- MAC pillar: pillar 4 (Irvine-baked).
from "local home baker might be casual" to "home kitchen can still be polished"
- Buyer tension: "Can I get this when I need it without a confusing process?"
- Insight: the whole experience should feel considered, from ordering to handoff.

### traceability for new content

Before publishing any new content, answer these questions:

1. Which content type does this map to in the skeleton table?
2. Which MAC pillar does it primarily activate?
3. Which belief shift does it move the buyer toward?
4. Which buyer tension or objection does it address?
5. Does the language follow the voice and vocabulary rules from the MAC?
6. Is texture, occasion, or a specific ingredient the lead element?

If any answer is unclear, revise the content before publishing.

## section 5: narrative maintenance

### review cadence

- **Monthly:** review Instagram content against the skeleton table. Are all four pillars represented? Is split-open texture content leading?
- **Quarterly:** review website copy against the conversion narrative. Does the hero, product page, and gifting page still match the four acts? Review the MAC for needed updates based on customer feedback and reviews.
- **Seasonally:** update the content skeleton for upcoming occasions. Add seasonal hooks to the calendar. Review outreach language for offices and corporate gifting.
- **Annually:** full audit of all customer-facing copy against the insight builder, MAC, conversion narrative, and narrative OS.

### when to update

- **New flavor launch:** add to flavor spotlight rotation. Update product pages, packaging insert, and allergen card.
- **New pickup location or delivery radius:** update all pickup and delivery FAQ content, website, Instagram highlights, and email templates.
- **Price change:** update website product pages, outreach materials, and any social content that mentions pricing.
- **Customer feedback reveals a new objection:** add it to the purchase friction table in the insight builder, create a message response, and reflect it in the website FAQ and social content.
- **Competitor landscape shifts:** review the MAC and conversion narrative for necessary adjustments. Do not attack competitors. Adjust positioning through proof, not comparison.

### narrative drift warnings

These are signals that the narrative has drifted and needs correction:

- **Texture language is disappearing.** If captions or website copy are leading with "gourmet," "premium," "delicious," or brand story instead of "crisp edges," "dense fudgy center," "thick 4oz," the narrative has drifted.
- **Packaging is not being mentioned.** If gift-ready boxes, flavor cards, and reheating instructions are absent from content for more than two weeks, the gifting hook is being lost.
- **Localness has become decorative.** If "Irvine" and "OC" appear as hashtags but not as real value (pickup windows, freshness cues, local discovery language), they are not doing the job.
- **Content is organized by flavor, not occasion.** If the content calendar is a rotation of the 10 flavors without occasion hooks, the buyer's actual use case is not being served.
- **Voice has gone corporate.** If copy sounds like a dessert brand that could be based anywhere and sold by anyone, the local, warm, specific voice has been lost.
- **Objections are not being answered.** If the website or social content does not address price, texture, sweetness, pickup, freshness, or allergens within the viewer's first few interactions, the narrative is not closing the gap.
