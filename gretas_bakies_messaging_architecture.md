# Greta's Bakies: messaging architecture

A working document. Written to be used, not framed.

---

## 1. brand soul

Greta's Bakies is one woman in an Irvine kitchen who got obsessed with cookies and figured out how to make the kind nobody around here is making. Thick. Two fingers tall. Soft middle, edges that hold. The cookies you remember from a specific trip to New York, except now they live ten minutes from your apartment.

The soul of the brand isn't the bakery. It's her standards. She reads labels at Whole Foods. She thinks most cookies in OC taste like a factory. She started baking because she wanted a cookie that was worth the calories, and when her friends kept asking her to make more, she got a cottage food permit and a pouch designer and went.

There is no startup story here. Just a woman who bakes, takes it seriously, and ships from her kitchen.

The brand should always sound like her. Not like a brand about her.

---

## 2. target customer

**Who she is**

She's 28 to 34. Lives somewhere between Irvine, Newport, Costa Mesa, Tustin. Probably makes around $90K to $160K herself or with a partner. Apartment or a first house. Works in something adjacent to design, tech, healthcare, or law. Has a Le Creuset she actually uses.

**How she shops**

She reads ingredient labels. Not because she's on a diet but because seed oils annoy her and she has opinions. She'll spend $14 on jam from the farmers market and feel fine about it. She won't spend $7 on a cookie that tastes like Costco. The price isn't the issue. The disappointment is.

**What she buys cookies for**

- A Friday night in. Tea, a show, one cookie that's actually worth it.
- A dinner party where she didn't make dessert and doesn't want to walk in with a grocery store box.
- A friend going through something. Cookies are the language.
- Friendsgiving. She's been assigned dessert and she's not making a pie.
- Her desk at work, Tuesday, 3 p.m., a small thing for herself.

**What she's tired of**

Cookies that crumble before she gets them home. Ingredient lists that read like a chemistry quiz. The word "artisan." Paying $8 for marketing. Dry centers. Bakeries that are all wood signage and no flavor.

**What she wants**

A cookie she trusts. From a person, not a brand. Thick enough to feel like a moment. Clean enough that she doesn't have to think about it. Pretty enough to gift without re-wrapping.

---

## 3. positioning statement

For the woman in Orange County who actually cares what she eats and who she buys from, Greta's Bakies is the thick, fudgy-centered cookie made by one person in an Irvine home kitchen using ingredients you'd recognize. Levain-style heft, no factory shortcuts, no wellness pretense. Just very good cookies from someone who takes them seriously.

## 4. voice profile

### archetype

**The friend who bakes.** Specifically: the friend whose kitchen you end up in. She's warm but she has standards. She'll tell you the cookie is good because it is, not because she's selling it. She notices texture, weight, the way butter behaves at room temperature. She doesn't perform.

Secondary archetype, used sparingly: **the quiet expert.** She knows what she's doing. She doesn't need to announce it.

### voice axes

Scored 1 to 10. These are the dials. Hold them.

| axis | score | what it means |
|---|---|---|
| warm ←→ cool | **7 warm** | warm, but not gushing. she's not your mom. |
| casual ←→ formal | **8 casual** | sounds like a text, not a brochure. |
| playful ←→ serious | **6 playful** | dry humor, occasional. never cute. never punny. |
| specific ←→ vague | **9 specific** | always name the thing. "the matcha" not "premium ingredients." |
| sensory ←→ analytical | **8 sensory** | lead with how it feels, looks, behaves. |
| confident ←→ humble | **7 confident** | she knows the cookies are good. she doesn't need to prove it. |
| indulgent ←→ restrained | **6 indulgent** | rich, but never excessive. one good adjective beats three. |

### tone modulations

The voice flexes by surface.

- **Packaging & pouch micro-copy:** drier, more observational. "Overheard in the bakery" energy. Time-stamped. Quiet.
- **Instagram captions:** warmer, more conversational. A person typing, not a brand posting.
- **Website body:** confident, plain, declarative. Sentences end early.
- **Order confirmation / replies to customers:** warmest setting. Like she's writing back to a friend. Still no exclamation points unless she means it.
- **Holiday / event copy:** slightly more occasion-aware, never sentimental. No "tis the season."

### voice characteristics

- **Texture first.** Always. The cookie is a physical object before it is anything else. Talk about the middle, the edge, the weight in your hand.
- **Plainspoken.** Short sentences. Real words. No copywriter vocabulary.
- **Observed, not claimed.** "You can see the matcha" beats "made with the finest matcha." Show the thing.
- **Comfortable with silence.** Not every line needs to sell. Some lines just sit there.
- **Specificity as flex.** Naming a brand, a temperature, a time, a street. Detail is the proof.
- **Quietly funny.** Once in a while. Never working for the laugh.

### signature moves

These are hers. Use them.

1. **The timestamp.** "10:47 a.m., second tray out." Locates the cookie in time. Makes the kitchen real.
2. **The texture lead.** Open with the feeling. "Soft in the middle, edge that holds." Then everything else.
3. **The ingredient callout, in passing.** "The green is matcha." "Real butter, the good kind." Casual, not boasted.
4. **The short line.** A sentence alone on a line. For weight. Like this.
5. **The honest no.** When something isn't there, say so plainly. "No food coloring, except the red velvet. Some things are worth keeping."
6. **The address-adjacent detail.** Irvine. A home kitchen. A pouch. Locating the brand in a specific physical place builds trust without trying.

### banned moves

These never appear. Non-negotiable.

- Em dashes. Zero. Use a comma, colon, period, or "and."
- Title case in headings. Sentence case only.
- "Not X but Y" framing. No contrarian setup. No "this isn't your average cookie."
- Triplet adjectives. Max two. "Dense and fudgy" yes. "Craggy, dense, and loaded" no.
- "Artisan," "handcrafted," "small-batch," "made with love," "crafted."
- "Premium," "best," "perfect," "ultimate."
- "Our secret recipe." Nobody cares about the secret. They care about the cookie.
- Wellness language. Clean, glow, guilt-free, treat yourself, balance, mindful. All gone.
- Generic superlatives of any kind.
- Exclamation points, by default. About one per month, if that.
- Emoji clusters. One emoji, occasionally, if it earns it.
- "Cookies that hit different." "Obsessed." "Game changer." Internet exhaust.
- Saccharine warmth. "We pour love into every cookie." She'd cringe.
- Apologizing for the price. The cookies are worth it.

## 5. message pillars

Four. These are the things the brand says, over and over, in different ways.

### pillar 1: the thickness

The physical fact of the cookie. Two inches tall. Mounded. Fudgy in the middle. This is the visual hero and the first thing anyone notices. Lead here on packaging, photography, and any first impression. Talk about weight, height, the soft center, the way the edge sets up.

*Sample language:* "Two inches tall. Soft in the middle. Holds its shape until the second you bite it."

### pillar 2: the kitchen, the person

The brand is one woman in Irvine. Not a team. Not a factory. The kitchen is real, the permit is real, the hands are hers. Use this pillar to build trust without performing humility. Reference the kitchen, the trays, the pouch, the timing. Never make it feel like a hardship story. She's good at this.

*Sample language:* "Baked this morning in a home kitchen in Irvine. Pouched warm, sealed cool."

### pillar 3: the ingredients you'd recognize

Clean ingredients, framed as a taste choice, not a health claim. The flex is that you can read the label. The food coloring rule is the recurring fun detail: everything green is matcha, everything pink is raspberry, the only food coloring is in the red velvet because red velvet is supposed to be red.

*Sample language:* "The green is matcha. The pink is raspberry. The red is food coloring, because red velvet is supposed to be red."

### pillar 4: cookies for the actual moments

Cookies as a real object in a real life. Friday night, dinner party, friend in a bad week, desk at work. Not "treat yourself" framing. Just naming the moments out loud so she sees herself.

*Sample language:* "For the Friday you stay in. For the friend who needs one. For the dinner you forgot dessert for."

## 6. packaging micro-copy: "overheard in the bakery"

The pouch is matte deep berry (P 77-8 C), 5 inches, editorial object. The micro-copy system gives it a small interior voice. Timestamps, fragments, the texture of a real morning in the kitchen. Never a tagline. Never a sales line. The pouch is allowed to be quiet.

### the rules

- One micro-copy line per pouch, max. Two only on hero SKUs.
- Time-stamped or location-stamped where possible.
- Present tense. Observational.
- 4 to 14 words. Almost always under 10.
- No verbs of marketing. (No "enjoy," "savor," "experience.")
- Lowercase by default, except proper nouns.

### sample lines (use, adapt, rotate)

- 7:42 a.m. first tray, still warm.
- the matcha is the matcha.
- two inches, give or take.
- soft middle. edge holds.
- baked this morning. eaten today, ideally.
- the red is food coloring. the rest isn't.
- pouched at 11. yours by Friday.
- the chocolate is real chocolate.
- everything green here is matcha.
- one woman, one oven, ten flavors.
- butter, sugar, eggs. the usual.
- made in Irvine. the kitchen, not the factory.
- 1.5 inches on a slow day. 2 on a good one.
- if it cracked a little on top, that's correct.

### back-of-pouch / interior card

This is where she can be slightly longer. Two to four short sentences. Plainspoken. Always signed off without a flourish.

*Template:*
> Thick cookies from a home kitchen in Irvine. Baked by one person, in small trays, the day before they ship. Ingredients you can read. The only food coloring is in the red velvet. Eat them within the week if you can.
>
Greta's Bakies, Irvine CA

## 7. tagline & byline options

No final pick. These are the candidates. Use the one that sits right.

### taglines (pick one, hold it)

1. **two inches of cookie.** (literal, confident, visual hero)
2. **thick cookies from Irvine.** (plain, locational, true)
3. **baked this morning.** (timestamped, intimate)
4. **the cookie is the point.** (quietly confident, anti-marketing)
5. **soft in the middle.** (texture-first, reads like a feeling)
6. **a home kitchen in Irvine.** (locating, builds trust)

Strongest two: **thick cookies from Irvine** and **two inches of cookie.** The first travels better. The second is more memorable.

### bylines (the small line under the logo)

- baked in Irvine, CA
- home kitchen, cottage food permit
- one oven, ten flavors
- thick, by design

### social bio drafts

- "thick cookies, made in Irvine. one kitchen, ten flavors. gretasbakies.com"
- "Irvine home kitchen. cookies are two inches tall. order at gretasbakies.com"

## 8. what not to say

A list to keep near the keyboard. If a line uses one of these, rewrite it.

- "Crafted with love."
- "Our secret family recipe."
- "Premium ingredients."
- "Small-batch artisan cookies."
- "The ultimate cookie experience."
- "Indulge yourself."
- "Treat yourself."
- "Guilt-free." (or any guilt framing, in either direction)
- "Clean eating."
- "Better-for-you."
- "Wholesome goodness."
- "Cookies that hit different."
- "Obsessed with these."
- "Game changer."
- "These aren't your average cookies."
- "Not your typical cookie."
- "We believe..." (any sentence starting with "we believe.")
- "Passionate about baking."
- "Born out of a love for..."
- "Made with the finest..."
- "A flavor experience."
- "Decadent."
- "Heavenly."
- "To die for."
- Any sentence with three adjectives in a row.
- Any sentence with an em dash.
- Any heading in Title Case.

## 9. voice check: before and after

Examples of how the voice fixes copy in real time. Use these as a calibration tool.

---

**before:**
> Indulge in our handcrafted, small-batch cookies, made with love in our Irvine kitchen using only the finest premium ingredients.

**after:**
> Thick cookies from a home kitchen in Irvine. Baked the day before they ship.

*Why:* removed "indulge," "handcrafted," "small-batch," "made with love," "finest premium," and the em dash. Kept the location, the kitchen, the freshness. Said less. Meant more.

---

**before:**
> Our matcha and macadamia cookie is a heavenly fusion of premium ceremonial-grade matcha and crunchy, buttery macadamia nuts. The perfect treat for any matcha lover!

**after:**
> Matcha and macadamia. The green is the matcha. The crunch is the nuts. Soft middle, edge that holds.

*Why:* led with the thing, named the ingredients without flexing the grade, used the signature move ("the green is the matcha"), no exclamation point, no superlative.

---

**before:**
> Treat yourself to a guilt-free indulgence! Our cookies use clean ingredients that nourish your body and soul.

**after:**
> Ingredients you can read. Butter, sugar, eggs, the usual. They taste like cookies because they are cookies.

*Why:* killed the wellness framing entirely. The clean-ingredients story is about taste and trust, not nourishment. Last sentence has the dry humor the voice can do once in a while.

---

**before:**
> Greta's Bakies was born out of a passion for creating the perfect cookie. We pour our hearts into every batch.

**after:**
> Greta's Bakies is one woman in an Irvine kitchen who got obsessed with cookies and got a permit to sell them.

*Why:* origin story without the violins. Specific (Irvine, kitchen, permit). The word "obsessed" earns it here because it's a description, not a hashtag.

---

**before:**
> Order now and experience cookies like never before!

**after:**
> Order by Wednesday. Pouched Friday. Eat them this weekend if you can.

*Why:* replaced a hype line with information. The voice trusts the cookie to do its own work.

---

**before:**
> Our Red Velvet cookie features a rich, decadent, velvety crumb with a hint of cocoa and a luxurious cream cheese drizzle.

**after:**
> Red velvet. Soft cocoa crumb, cream cheese on top. The red is food coloring, because red velvet is supposed to be red.

*Why:* two adjectives max, not five. Used the food-coloring rule as the recurring honest detail. Sentence-case heading. No "luxurious."

---

## 10. one-page summary for the owner

If you only remember six things:

1. Lead with texture. Always say how the cookie feels before what's in it.
2. Sound like a person, not a brand. Short sentences. Real words.
3. No em dashes, no title case, no "artisan," no "made with love," no superlatives.
4. The food-coloring rule is your recurring honest detail. Use it.
5. When in doubt, say less. The cookie does the work.
6. You're a woman in Irvine who bakes very good cookies. Write like that.

end of brief
