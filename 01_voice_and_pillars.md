# Greta's Bakies: voice and message pillars

This version moves the brand away from operational claims and into emotional reasons to care. The cookies are still thick and local, with gift-ready pouches and ingredient clarity, although the copy should lead with the feeling those facts create: the pull of the middle and the relief of showing up with something good.

 - -

## voice profile

### voice role

Greta's Bakies should sound like the friend in Orange County who always knows where the good thing is, and who happens to be the person baking it. She is warm because she is a real person, and she is confident because the cookie has a crisp edge, a dense middle, and enough weight to make the first bite feel like an event.

The voice should feel like a text from someone with taste. It can be funny and a little dry, although it should never sound like a brand trying to be charming. The cookie does most of the work, so the copy points to the real details and lets people want it.

### how the voice behaves

- **Texture comes first:** Lead with the soft middle and the set edge before naming the flavor.
- **Specific details do the selling:** Say Irvine and 4 oz when scale or location matters.
- **Warmth stays grounded:** Write like a person who would hand you a cookie across the counter and tell you which one to eat warm.
- **Indulgence stays honest:** These are butter-and-sugar cookies, so the job is to make the pleasure feel worth it.
- **Trust is shown through proof:** Ingredient clarity and a home kitchen in Irvine should feel natural rather than rehearsed.
- **The rhythm breathes:** Use short lines for packaging and longer connected sentences for captions or web copy.

### language rules

- No em dashes.
- Use sentence case in headings.
- Avoid contrarian setups and category-putdown lines.
- Avoid health-coded positioning and any promise that makes cookies sound like health food.
- Avoid familiar bakery clichés and any language that sounds mass-produced.
- Keep lists tight, with two items when possible.
- Prefer concrete product truths over abstract claims.
- Let the brand feel elevated through restraint and proof.

### signature moves

1. **The texture admission:** "The middle is the reason."
2. **The real-life occasion:** "Bring these when you said you'd handle dessert."
3. **The honest ingredient note:** "The green is matcha, and the pink is raspberry."
4. **The local proof:** "Baked in Irvine, picked up before dinner."
5. **The indulgence wink:** "This is so good, you can tell nobody tried to make it behave."
6. **The people line:** "For the gooey-middle cookie people."

## message pillars

The brand should repeat these six ideas in different ways across packaging and pickup reminders, with captions and product pages carrying the longer explanation. Each pillar gives Greta's Bakies a feeling to own instead of a feature to list.

 - -

### pillar 1: gooey people only

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies makes thick cookies for people who care most about the soft middle and the first warm bite.

**Why it matters:** Cookie people have strong opinions, and they know the difference between a cookie that looks good online and a cookie that gives way when you bite into it. This pillar makes the texture a tribe, because the center is the thing her customer remembers and orders again.

**Sample lines:**

- For the gooey-middle cookie people.
- The middle is the point.
- Crisp edge and soft center, full stop.
- The bite starts at the edge and ends in the middle.
- Thick enough to make you pause before sharing.
- If you warm it for eight seconds, you will understand.
- The outside holds together and the inside gives in.
- Some people want crunch. These are for the middle people.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants a cookie that feels special enough to justify the price, and she wants proof before she commits.

**The belief it shifts:** Thickness becomes more than size, because it becomes the promise of a dense, fudgy center.

**Proof points:**

- Each cookie is 4 oz and roughly 1.5 to 2 inches tall.
- The style is Levain-inspired, with set edges and a dense middle.
- Flavors such as Triple Chocolate and Biscoff naturally show the center.
- Product photos can hero the split cookie and the pull.

 - -

### pillar 2: worth the last bite

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies gives her customer a cookie that feels worth the calories and the drive.

**Why it matters:** Her customer already knows she wants dessert, and she wants dessert that delivers because she has been disappointed by pretty cookies that taste flat and oversized cookies that eat dry. This pillar lets the brand own the private little calculation people already make: if I am going to have a cookie, it should be this one.

**Sample lines:**

- This is so good, you can tell nobody tried to make it behave.
- A cookie with consequences, in the best way.
- The kind you split, then regret splitting.
- Worth eating slowly over the sink.
- Buy six and pretend the plan was sharing.
- It tastes like someone took the cookie seriously.
- The Friday night cookie should carry the night.
- If dessert is happening, make it count.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants indulgence without feeling like she paid for hype.

**The belief it shifts:** A cookie can be rich and restrained at the same time, because the pleasure comes from texture and timing.

**Proof points:**

- Dense centers keep the cookie from eating dry.
- Real flavor cues show up clearly, such as matcha in the green cookie and raspberry in the pink one.
- Ten flavors create choice without making the menu feel random.
- The matte deep berry pouch makes the cookie feel considered before it is opened.

### pillar 3: gifted before you wrap it

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies helps people show up with dessert that feels thoughtful before they add a card.

**Why it matters:** The target customer cares about the arrival, and a plastic grocery clamshell rarely feels like the right entrance. She may be coming straight from work or pickup, so this pillar makes the pouch and the local origin feel like social relief because the cookies already look like she made a good call.

**Sample lines:**

- Bring these when you said you'd handle dessert.
- Looks like effort, which is the whole point.
- Pretty enough to hand over as is.
- For the friend who needs the good cookie.
- Dinner party dessert, handled before parking.
- The pouch does the wrapping for you.
- A better answer than "I brought wine."
- Put them on a plate and take the compliment.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants to be generous and tasteful, although she may be coming straight from work or pickup.

**The belief it shifts:** Store-bought can still feel personal when the object has weight and a real local maker behind it.

**Proof points:**

- Matte deep berry pouches create a giftable first impression.
- Four-ounce cookies feel substantial enough for sharing or plating.
- Local pickup makes last-minute hosting easier within Irvine and nearby OC.
- Flavor range covers familiar choices and more interesting picks, such as Matcha + Macadamia or Caramel + Pretzel.

 - -

### pillar 4: the label can come with us

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies uses recognizable ingredients and honest flavor cues, so customers can buy dessert without side-eyeing the label.

**Why it matters:** Her customer reads labels because she pays attention, and she still wants to know that the flavor came from real things wherever possible. This pillar keeps ingredient consciousness grounded in taste and trust, so the brand can say butter and sugar are doing exactly what cookies need them to do.

**Sample lines:**

- The green is matcha, and the pink is raspberry.
- The red velvet is red because red velvet should be red.
- Butter and sugar are doing their jobs here.
- Read the label, then eat the cookie.
- The chocolate tastes like chocolate because that was the assignment.
- Ingredients you can recognize, with the cookie still acting like dessert.
- The apple cookie tastes like apple instead of candle.
- Clear enough to trust and rich enough to be a cookie.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants to care about ingredients while still wanting a real dessert.

**The belief it shifts:** Ingredient clarity can stay out of diet culture because it can simply make the cookie taste better and feel more trustworthy.

**Proof points:**

- The brand has no organic certification, so the copy should avoid organic claims.
- Red Velvet is the only flavor with food coloring.
- The menu has visible flavor logic, with familiar chocolate choices and fruit or nut flavors doing real work.
- Cottage food labeling can be plain and confidence-building.

### pillar 5: Irvine has cookie people

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies makes the kind of thick cookie people usually think they have to leave Orange County to find.

**Why it matters:** Orange County has plenty of polished dessert, although the customer still knows when something tastes like a chain or a photo prop. This pillar gives the brand a local chip on its shoulder in a quiet way, because the thrill is realizing the cookie she wanted lives close enough for pickup.

**Sample lines:**

- Thick cookies from Irvine, finally.
- Baked close enough to pick up before dinner.
- Orange County has middle-cookie people too.
- The cookie you wanted after a New York trip, now near home.
- Made in an Irvine kitchen with a very serious oven schedule.
- For Newport birthdays and Costa Mesa Fridays.
- Local dessert should still feel like a find.
- Your friend in Tustin is going to ask where these came from.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants something with taste and specificity, and she wants it from a place that feels connected to her life.

**The belief it shifts:** Local can feel current and useful, and it can still be worth telling people about.

**Proof points:**

- Greta's Bakies operates from an Irvine home kitchen under a Class A cottage food permit.
- Direct-to-consumer pickup makes the location part of the story.
- The brand can reference real OC moments such as dinner in Costa Mesa or a Friday night in Newport.
- The founder is visible enough to build trust without turning every caption into a personal diary.

### pillar 6: dessert with a calendar

**Core promise:** Greta's Bakies gives people a cookie for the actual moments that need dessert or a small plan.

**Why it matters:** Cookies become more desirable when they attach to real life. The customer buys them for Friday nights and dinner parties, plus desk afternoons and the friend who insists she is fine. This pillar makes the brand easy to remember because it gives the cookie a job in the week.

**Sample lines:**

- For Friday night, when dinner was snacks.
- For the friend who says she is fine.
- For the 3 p.m. desk cookie you planned at 10 a.m.
- For birthdays where cake feels like too much.
- For the dinner party you remembered at lunch.
- For the group chat that keeps asking what to bring.
- For the night you want tea and one very serious cookie.
- For the thank-you that needs to taste like you meant it.

**The tension it resolves:** She wants a reason to buy before the craving becomes an impulse.

**The belief it shifts:** Cookies can become the dessert plan or the gesture that makes the week softer.

**Proof points:**

- Pouches travel well for gifting and dinner plans.
- Ten flavors let the customer match cookies to people and occasions.
- Four-ounce size makes one cookie feel like a full dessert.
- Direct ordering helps customers plan pickup around the week.

## how to use the pillars by channel

### packaging

Use the shortest lines from "gooey people only" and "gifted before you wrap it." Packaging should feel like a small aside from the kitchen, so lines can be dry and under ten words when space is tight.

### instagram

Use the emotional truth from each pillar and write like Greta is explaining the cookie to someone who already likes dessert. Captions can be longer when they connect texture to a real moment, such as a Friday pickup or the first batch of Matcha + Macadamia coming out of the oven.

### website

Use "gooey people only" as the first impression, then support it with "the label can come with us" and "Irvine has cookie people." The website should make the product easy to understand in the first few seconds, and then it should make the buyer feel smart for choosing it.

### product pages

Start each product description with texture, then mention the flavor detail that proves the name. Chocolate Chip can lead with the soft center and chocolate pockets, while Matcha + Macadamia can lead with the green matcha crumb and nut crunch.

### gifting and holidays

Use "gifted before you wrap it" and "dessert with a calendar" so seasonal copy stays useful instead of sentimental. The strongest lines name the exact social problem the cookies solve, such as showing up with dessert or sending something to a friend.

## quick owner summary

Greta's Bakies should own thick cookies with a soft center, although the deeper brand is about people who know when a cookie is actually worth it. The new pillars give the brand six repeatable emotional territories, from texture people to real-life occasions.

The best lines feel like something a customer would say after tasting the cookie. "For the gooey-middle cookie people" should become a signature, while "This is so good, you can tell nobody tried to make it behave" gives the brand a playful indulgence lane without sounding health-coded or hyped up.
